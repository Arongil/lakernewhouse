Benita Katzenellenbogen

Interview Transcript - May 2025
Interviewed by Laker Newhouse

Benita: I know nothing beyond my grandparents, Benjamin and Yetta. And I’m named for both of them, you know. Benita was my mother’s creation.
Laker: Wow. And your name is close to “beautiful” in several languages.
Benita: Yes, that’s right. And this is Miriam’s parents, Benjamin and Yetta. My mother had two siblings: Anna and Julius, and she was the youngest. Anna was the eldest, then Julius—Uncle Jack—was the middle. And he was very special in my life. He and his wife, Maidie—though her real name was Selma, but she went with Maidie—she was tiny, maybe 4’10”, quite petite, and very beautiful, I think, and very sweet. For reasons I don’t know, they didn’t have children. I think Jack thought she was too small, maybe it would compromise her health, which I don’t think was true at all. Plenty of people manage. But in any event, they really became like second parents to Joe and me. They lived maybe a 15-minute car ride away, or a half-hour by bus. So Joe and I, when we were older, would go over there by bus. This was in the days when everything was safe, you know, and he was a physician—a general practitioner, trained at NYU Medical—and she was a nurse. I don’t know exactly how they met—perhaps through work. But she was his nurse, also. He took care of people in the area. They were loving to me and my brother—all of them. That was the same on my father’s side. I never actually met my mother’s parents, unfortunately. Her mother died at 50, as my mother was graduating Hunter College, approximately. Benjamin was alive when Joe was born—he was delighted to have a grandson. But he passed away, I think, just a few months before I was born. So I never met grandparents on that side. On my father’s side, both my grandparents were alive—the first one passed when I was in college. So I had quite a long time to get to know them. They lived about 20 minutes away from us in Brooklyn. My grandfather, Louis Schulman, was kind but quite distant. His wife, Annie, was very sweet.
Laker: So your father had two siblings?
Benita: Yes, a sister, Celia, and a brother, David. David was a cardiologist and Celia was a high school teacher. She taught business courses, which was very different from today. I remember Paul Anka actually became a very famous singer, and he was one of her students—she taught him business math or something. Celia had one daughter. David had two children—Ronnie and Gilbert. Ronnie, as in Ronnie Schwimmer, Larry’s mother—that’s my first cousin. My other first cousin was Sue Schulman—she married quite young, her high school boyfriend Riley Hausdorff, and had four children. Her husband came from a more conservative Jewish background.
Laker: Was your mother’s side more secular?
Benita: Very much so. My mother’s family were Jewish, knew they were Jewish, but did nothing much Jewish. Very casual about it. My father’s family was more conservative, edging toward Orthodox when they first came to the U.S. In fact, my grandfather Louis Schulman was one of nine!
Laker: Wow.
Benita: Yes—big families were common then. Some of his sisters ended up in Newburgh, as did my mother’s sister Anna—she married a relative, Reuben Schulman. They were quite conservative in politics—Republican. Their son, Marvin Schulman, went to Yale and was head of the Young Yale Republicans—caused some lively arguments at family gatherings, since our branch was the more liberal side.
Laker: Can you walk us through the story of how both sides of your family came to the U.S.?
Benita: My understanding is that my grandmother Yetta Fitterman probably was of Russian background, but I’m not sure. Benjamin was Russian—at least, lived in Russia. But I suspect, like many Jews, they actually came from Austria or Hungary, and moved because times were hard. Benjamin, like many young Jewish men, was conscripted into the Russian army. He didn’t want to serve—he actually escaped in a laundry truck from the barracks, risking Siberia or worse if caught. He made his way to the U.S., where there was some family in New York—probably distantly related. He became a furrier, someone who sells or works with fur coats—mink, sable, that sort of thing. Actually, one of our relatives was Helena Rubinstein—she’s the founder of Rubinstein Women's Cosmetics.
Laker: That’s her?
Benita: Yes, I believe so—a second cousin, maybe a first cousin? And Benjamin and Yetta met in New York, though she was probably also born outside the U.S.
Laker: And your father’s family?
Benita: Both were from Austria-Hungary, not a great place for Jews at the time. They came to the U.S. as maybe eight-year-olds or early teenagers. I know nothing about their parents, unfortunately. Louis started a shirt manufacturing company, made a lot of money, and retired at 47—quite young. But then the Depression hit, the assets hadn’t been invested wisely, and—well, those were hard times. I think a real estate venture went surprisingly well though—prime real estate, worth millions now.
Laker: Before we hop to college, what are some defining memories from your childhood?
Benita: Many happy weekends with Aunt Maidie and Uncle Jack. From age ten on, I’d sleep over at their apartment. Sometimes Joe too, but usually just one of us. Aunt Maidie would make silver dollar pancakes on Sunday—tiny pancakes. We’d go to Prospect Park—they lived nearby, on the boulevard with the horse path. Uncle Jack and Aunt Maidie were big parts of my childhood. Also my Aunt Celia only had one daughter, Sue, was very sweet and I’d sometimes stay over at her place. Uncle Dave, the cardiologist, his wife’s family owned a dude ranch in Pennsylvania. I’d go there in the summers, sometimes with my grandmother, and we’d share a room.
Laker: What was it like to be Benita Schulman in elementary, middle, and high school?
Benita: I lived in a nice part of Brooklyn, Madison-Midwood—probably 90% Jewish, 10% Protestant or Catholic. For a while, I thought the whole world was Jewish! I went to public schools all the way through college, but the quality was excellent—teachers were outstanding, or at least some were. My friends were mostly intelligent—Barry Simon always sat behind me, since Schulman and Simon were next in the alphabet. I’d always see his hand popping up—“I know! I know!” He was quite a character. Summers, we’d have three-week family vacations—Cape Cod, Wellfleet, Buzzards Bay, New Hampshire. Once we went to Washington, D.C.—for me, the fun was the motel pool in Virginia. Joe actually was the one to tell me about sex—he was very matter-of-fact, said, “You should know about this.” I was twelve; he was right. My parents actually had some sex education books for teenagers just sitting on the shelf. My mother was a big supporter—though not in all ways. Before I met John, I was actually engaged to someone else—a lovely guy, a med student at Downstate in New York. It could have worked, but something was missing. He didn’t really see why I’d want to go to grad school. I was engaged for three weeks and just knew it wasn’t right. I told Joe, and he was supportive—said it was my decision. My mother said, “You’ll have to sleep with this person and share a life. If it doesn’t feel right, it’s OK.” My father, on the other hand, said, “He’s so lovely! What’s wrong with him?” I couldn’t really explain—it just wasn’t right. I gave back the ring, the silver, the gifts. We’d even had a party and guests. Arnie—his name was actually Armand, with a French spelling—was a twin, nice family, but I just couldn’t go through with it. He wasn’t happy, but maybe he sensed it.
Laker: That’s a very powerful example.
Benita: Yes. And I always had support from my mother in that domain, because she had also had suitors her parents liked but she knew were wrong. My Aunt Anna tried to set—
Laker: And your academics were very strong in college. You were very interested?
Benita: Yes, I did well in the GREs, essay writing, that sort of thing. Was it unusual for women to be in STEM fields then? Biology had more women than chemistry. Even now, chemistry isn’t 50-50, though it’s changing. Many went into high school teaching, some to med school. My circle had big aspirations. I had a friend, Nancy Rausch, went to Yale, did developmental biology, worked for Clement Market who was a big name then. There was pressure for women to marry early, meet a nice guy. After the “to-be-married” fell through, I started dating Alan Stephen Kaye, the school paper editor and a political science major. Dynamic, interesting. He went to Princeton, I was going to Harvard—I’d chosen it over Brandeis and others since Joe was already there. The one way my parents didn’t support me was financial. They said they’d pay for Joe to go to med school, but not for me to go to grad school. I said, “I’ll get a fellowship,” and they said, “Well, you’ll have to figure it out.” Support for the boy but not the girl. My Uncle Jack said, on the spot, “If she needs support, I’ll pay for her.” He wouldn’t put up with the difference—very sweet.
Laker: I’m curious about how you met Grandpa John—and then, moving forward, about how you built a family, your insights into parenting, and then advice for me.
Benita: At the end of my senior year, I was still involved with Alan. Maybe if he’d asked me to marry him, I’d have said yes—but he went to Princeton, I went to Harvard. On my first or second day in biochemistry class, John Katzenellenbogen sat down next to me. I thought he seemed very nice, and he was impressed I remembered his name—I had studied German. We became fond of each other, started studying together, playing ping pong—well. Once, his pants split when he bent down to pick up a ball. Thank goodness we were already dating! Too funny. That fall, Alan visited me at Harvard—unexpectedly with an engagement ring. I was flabbergasted. Things had changed by then—I realized I had to end it. That was a bad weekend, especially for him. After that, John and I became more serious. Maybe he told you—he was supposed to go to Columbia, but changed his mind and stayed at Harvard. At first, Corey didn’t know why, but he eventually realized it was because of a girl across the street in the Biolabs. John was always so sweet.
Laker: Can you tell me what it was like when you moved to Illinois, especially about parenting Debbie and Rachel?
Benita: Yes! We both went to Illinois with people saying, “Great place to start.” We thought we’d only be there five or seven years, but stayed. It was different—much more residential than big city, though not high-rises. I was extremely fond of John’s mother. She really modeled for me a person devoted to her work—her piano teaching, music. Loved gardening, too. My mother was probably better with children, but John’s mother was always warm, welcoming—wrote me a “welcome to the family” letter when we were engaged. I always wanted to have children—I wanted four, actually, but realized two was more practical. Maybe if I had more energy, I’d have tried for four. After three years married, we felt it was time. John wanted children, though he worried, “I don’t know if I’d be good enough as a father.” But he was! He’s patient, reads with the kids, just wonderful. His mother also read a lot—Rollo May, The Road Less Traveled. She was a tough, committed woman, dealt with immigrating, giving up a career for a while, but always kind. My parents loved the girls, too. My father retired earlier than expected, so they sometimes came up from Florida to babysit Debbie and Rachel when John and I went to conferences.
Laker: You also had a fellowship to work with some of the greats in your field, right?
Benita: Yes, I could work with Jack Gorski at Illinois or Elwood Jensen at University of Chicago—both discovered the estrogen receptor. John’s chemistry and my biological research was distinct, but we had some collaborative projects, which brought a lot of pleasure over the years. Students from his lab would interact with those from mine. We still have compounds being shared across the street! We had some very novel projects—thanks to that chemistry connection.
Laker: Any favorite stories from those years—either in the lab or, say, the famous “potty seat” incident?
Benita: We always included the girls in our science community. When someone visited to give a seminar, we’d have them to the house for cheese and crackers and drinks. The girls were always there. John enjoyed cooking—chicken paprikas, Greek dishes. One time, Debbie, who was potty training, proudly carried her little potty from the kitchen into the living room where guests were sitting, sat down, and made the biggest bowel movement ever, right there, very proud of herself. Another time, Rachel kept eating peanuts while our backs were turned—and, well, they all came right back up. She’d just been swallowing them whole. But we also took them to conferences and meetings—one of the first was the East-West meeting in Honolulu. Later, they came with us to Paris, to Copenhagen. We wanted to introduce them—and ourselves—to the European side of the family. They even started picking up some Swiss-German from John’s relatives. We stayed in Illinois because it worked so well—we could bike to work, schools were good, the public education system solid at a time when many places were struggling. Really, we were lucky.
Laker: I’m struck by how you blended science, family, travel, and got help so you could do both work and parenting.
Benita: Well, you asked about advice. First, don’t put off having children forever, if you want them—because as you advance in your research, you get busier and have more responsibility. Plus, you have more energy when you’re younger, and fewer fertility problems. Finding the right time is different for every couple, but I didn’t mind that much of my salary went toward caregivers—it was an investment in our family’s future, and John felt the same. So when my women students—sheepishly—confide, “Dr. K, I’m expecting in five months,” I say, “That’s great! You’re the right age to have a child. Don’t hesitate to get all the help you can—yes, it might take a big chunk of your salary, but it’s worth it.” Almost all of them are so happy afterward. On marriage—I always tell people, when someone asks, “How did you know John was the right person?”—it’s a leap of faith. You just feel it’s right, and that was the best decision I ever made. His caring spirit really showed after my father died—he helped my mother with so much as she got older and weaker. He couldn’t have been a better son-in-law. I do think kindness, intelligence, even how people interact—all those get passed down, if only indirectly. You don’t sit down with a four-year-old and say, “Be kind,”—but they watch how you treat one another. Our girls learned a lot by visiting friends—like the French family in France. Debbie, who was brave enough to go alone first, called us once and said, “The parents fight a lot. I’ve never seen that before. It’s so upsetting.” So even going on a sleepover can open your eyes—shows you the world is different outside, but also teaches you how you want your life to be. At the same time, I don’t think you should shield kids from everything—otherwise they don’t learn to process it or decide how to respond themselves. I was disappointed that Joe's marriage didn't work out with Carol. They had many things in common that they did enjoy, but there were also deep-seated problems, unfortunately. The fact that half of marriages in the U.S. end in divorce means that maybe some of your relatives will end up in that scenario. But there are examples of people being adaptable, growing together. At Debbie and Eric's wedding, John and I had the more usual statements: "We love both of you, we hope your marriage will get richer with time." Then Meg spoke, and I was almost shell-shocked. It was about how marriage can be very difficult. You learn that you have to stick together as best you can, including through hard times. It was advice, but advice I had never heard at any wedding by the parents. And it is true in every marriage, even ones that look perfect from the outside, that things are not perfect. But as much as possible, if two people feel like they are in an equal partnership, that can make for the most joyful relationship.
Laker: There's so much I admire about you as a mother, as a scientist, and especially as a grandmother. I didn't know some of the stories.
Benita: My only last thought is I'm so grateful to have you be my grandson. I wish you a very happy and joyful life.
