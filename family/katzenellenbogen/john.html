John Katzenellenbogen

Interview Transcript - May 2025
Interviewed by Laker Newhouse

Laker: Maybe you could start with stories from ancestors that mean a lot to you, then some life stories of your own, and we can save a couple minutes for advice you might want to give me.
John: All right, let’s start. Stories from the past. I thought about this a little last night. Starting with my father and mother, and their ancestry… Our family relocated to the U.S. before I was born, so I never had direct contact with my grandparents. I was in the youngest generation, born late to my father, who was the last to get married. I was the second child; my sister was born in Switzerland. My father would tell me some things, but not an awful lot. He did say that when he was growing up, his father was a very successful banker in Frankfurt, Germany, with many clients—quite wealthy from good business deals. My father liked him and admired him, but he was conflicted. He wasn’t interested in business, but thought his father would expect him to carry on the family business, or something like it. So he went through his rigorous education—classical studies, then professional training in law. He got a law degree from a distinguished German university. Then he told his father, “Now I’m prepared to do whatever you’d like me to do.” His father said, “Well, what would you like to do?” And he said, “To tell you the truth, I’ve always wanted to be a classicist or an art historian.” And his father said, “Why didn’t you tell me that in the first place!” My father had already arranged, pending his father’s permission, to do a PhD with a distinguished medievalist German art historian, Erwin Panofsky, which he did, and he was the last PhD in Germany that Panofsky took on before Panofsky went to the US. Anyway, being a Jew in Germany, even though fully integrated and not practicing, it was difficult for my father to get any job. He was a corporate lawyer for a while, but eventually was let go. He and my mother lived in Konstanz; she commuted to Switzerland to give piano lessons, and he just hoped the Nazis would lose political power and he could resume what he wanted to do in Germany. Some friends left. Some said, “You should leave.” But he thought he could stick it out. There were reasons he thought that way. In hindsight, maybe not the best call. Anyway, the advice my father gave me was about parental expectations—don’t feel like you have to follow in the footsteps of your parents. His father was a banker, but he became an art historian. He knew I really wasn’t interested in art, though I did pick up a few things from him. I liked hearing the animated discussions among all the displaced German academicians, many of whom had settled in New York City. They’d come to our house in Poughkeepsie for visits and would talk for hours, completely enthralled by what they were doing. But I was interested in science. That was one message I got from my father. The other was related to the whole dreadful experience he had had with the Nazis. My father really went through hell—taken away, put in a concentration camp in the middle of the German winter, and released by a miracle of action by my mother, nearly dead from double pneumonia. That’s probably why they let him out—he was about to die. He was nursed back to health, and, fortuitously, he was able to make it to America. Panofsky, who’d settled in Princeton at the Institute for Advanced Studies, arranged a visiting traveling fellowship for him. That’s how my father got to the U.S., toured for a year giving lectures, and on his way back to Europe when the war broke out—again, by luck—at his very last stop he got a position at Vassar College. Do you know the rest of the story? He got citizenship by going to Cuba and coming in under the Cuba quota. Each country had quotas—Cuba's was the only one not filled.
Laker: Yes, I remember some of that.
John: Then my mother and my sister, who was born a year before he left, were able, again almost miraculously, to get a visa and come to the U.S.—which was very, very difficult to do. The family was reunited. They decided not to tell me or my sister—who was seven years older—about the burdens and suffering they’d endured. It would’ve been too much for a youngster to process. But there were lessons. For example, when we lived in Baltimore, I found a book entitled Eugenics in a used bookstore. I didn’t know what eugenics was. The introduction said eugenics was the belief that mankind could be improved by having the smartest people do most of the breeding. I was intrigued and told my father. He just said, “That’s what the Nazis believed.” Nothing more. It shocked me—this was the idea that nearly got him killed. I didn’t read the book. Later on, I learned about the horrible history of eugenics in the U.S., including forced sterilizations and very ugly political movements. The Germans really took it to extremes and used it against all their enemies. It was another lesson—how ideas and technology, like those IBM punch cards, which was used by the Nazi’s for tracking their victims, could turn dark. Those were the two messages from my father: don’t feel pressure to follow your parents’ path, and be wary of seductive but dangerous ideas. Both were delivered in an indirect but very educational way. As for my mother’s side—she came from a much simpler, but not undistinguished, Swiss family. Her father was a master carpenter—made wonderful furniture. In fact, the desk I use in my study was made by him. His name was Holzheu, which means, I think, “wood builder,” or “wood chopper.” My mother was the eldest of four. She loved her mother, who died young. Without contraception, women had more children than they could bear. After three children, she had an abortion that became fatal. All the community knew it was coming; the men waited in a bar for the bad news, and the women gathered together. When she died, it was devastating, but sadly this was not uncommon at the time.
Laker: I want to ask about a couple of family traditions before we move on from ancestry. Can you explain what “Familie zurückhalten" means?
John: Oh, yes. So, my grandfather would host big meals at their summer house outside Frankfurt, in Königstein. That’s where the family kept the goat for my father, by the way. He was sickly and undernourished as a child, and his pediatrician recommended that he drink goat’s milk. Why buy the milk when you can buy a goat! When my grandfather was entertaining guests, the children used to go into the kitchen and talk with the cook. They’d try to find out which dishes were “limited edition”—the ones there may not be enough of for everyone. So, when a dish like that was being served to family and guests, the kids would softly chant, “Eff tzet, eff tzet!” (“F, Z” in German), which meant “Familie zurückhalten,” or “family hold back.” It was their way to signal: don’t take a huge portion, let the guests get enough. They thought it was hilarious.
Laker: And the second ham story?
John: Ah, yes—the classic. At another big festival meal, the cook came in with a huge ham on a tray. She slipped, and the ham fell on the floor. My grandmother, calm as can be, said, "Please pick up the ham, take it to the kitchen, and bring out the other ham." Sure enough, a moment later, out came another ham—looking suspiciously like the one that had just hit the floor. Everyone ate it happily. I’ve had similar moments, grilling steaks and dropping them on the deck. Picked off a few leaves, brushed them off, and served them up. Sometimes you don’t want to see too closely what’s under your food!
Laker: Wonderful. I’d love to fast-forward a little. What was your childhood in America like, and how did your interest in science develop?
John: When I was born, we’d just moved into our second place, a small, rather primitive house. No central heating—my father had to shovel coal into a big hopper for the furnace, which fed into the fire by auger. He kept that fire burning all winter. The hot water came from a tank you’d heat by lighting a fire underneath, using large chunks of coal. Nothing in the house worked very well, so I learned early how to fix things. My mother likes to tell this story: The kitchen door wouldn’t shut because they’d painted over the latch. I found a hammer and a screwdriver even before I could really walk, went over, and managed to hit the latch until it popped out and the door clicked. My parents were terrified—they thought I’d kill myself with the tools—but I fixed it. I became the family’s “mechanical genius.” Another time, when I was very young and could barely talk, I was home alone one summer and saw the coal-fired water heater was leaking. I ran next door to old Mr. Kane, who had a shop and taught me a lot about tools, and dragged him over: “The water’s running! The water’s running!” He turned it off for me, and we got a new (finally gas-fired) water heater. On the subject of keeping things hot—and cold—there was the Kelvinator story. Originally, we had an icebox, then a terrible old refrigerator that only half-worked. When we moved again it did not work at all, but one of the movers claimed he could fix it for $20 (a lot at the time). He did something—it started up and he left with the money, but then it stopped working almost immediately. We bought a brand new fridge—a Kelvinator. The green door had all the letters for K-E-L-V-I-N-A-T-O-R, and my father and sister, who loved to make acronyms, sat there puzzling over it. Eventually, they came up with: “Katzenellenbogens Employ Louie. Very Inefficient. Not Able To Overhaul Refrigerator.” Or maybe, “Overhaul Rubbish!” My father always kept a cheerful spirit and loved things like that.
Laker: What was high school like?
John: I’d skipped a grade in elementary school, so I ended up starting at the Gilman School, a private boys’ school in Baltimore, for what would have been ninth grade. First form, second form… They did things by “forms” instead of grades. I had to brush up on algebra—no problem—but I also needed to learn Latin. Luckily, my father was fluent, and drilled me daily until I caught up. My classmates were mostly sons of Baltimore’s business and professional elite. Most were not very intellectual, which was disappointing, but the rigorous curriculum prepared me well for college placement. Their recommendation opened doors at Harvard, and I was the only one in several years to go there—most headed to Princeton for easy degrees, then home to work in the family business, or the girlfriend’s father’s business, the whole transition named “The Fur Lined Furrow.” I went off to Harvard uncertain what I’d find. I've told you about those first challenges—especially writing. In high school, I’d been fine by writing a lot, but in college, you needed real efficiency, say more with fewer words. I remember the study test: read a huge chapter, then summarize it in 10 minutes—impossible unless you noticed the summary paragraphs in the margins and the big chapter summary at the end. I completely failed, so I took a remedial class that was actually great. It taught me how to survive in college: you have to learn to see the whole structure of a course, not just grind away getting lost in generally irrelevant details.
Laker: What about your social life—dating, friendships?
John: Tried the usual pattern: I'd date a girl, she’d meet my roommates, and then one of them would “pick her off.” Happened twice. In the end, it was a blessing. My senior year, feeling pretty confident walking to biochemistry class, I met a young woman from New York. Benita Schulman. We met in lecture, and I asked to sit next to her. The next time, I said, “I bet you don’t remember my name.” She replied, “Of course, you’re John Katzenellenbogen.” That showed some quick intelligence, and it appealed to me. It worked well—biochemistry was easy for me, but challenging for biology majors like her. I helped her a lot. She wasn’t exclusive at first; she had others she was seeing. At one point, she almost called it off, but I found ways to remind her I might have other options too—nothing serious, but enough to let her know! Eventually, we got back together. I don’t tell this part to many people—Benita might be crestfallen—but during a rough patch, I had offers from Harvard, Columbia, and Stanford for grad school. I picked Columbia, not wanting to stay at Harvard if Benita was there and things were awkward. But when we worked things out, I reversed my decision. I had to write to Columbia from Greece—very dramatic—apologizing, and wait for their reply before telling Harvard I’d accept them after all. Luckily, the Harvard professor I wanted to work with vouched for me, so it worked out. I started grad school at Harvard, then we married after my first year. Benita was a year ahead of me in school, but I finished my PhD faster since I knew the professor, had taken all the graduate classes, and could hit the ground running. I did spend a month in the hospital with infectious hepatitis from undercooked steamed clams at the welcome-for-new-students picnic—so now I only eat them well-cooked!
Laker: I’d love to hear a bit about the years right after that—parenting, philosophies, and maybe some stories about raising Debbie and Rachel.
John: After Benita and I both finished our degrees, I got a position at Illinois, and we moved. Benita got a great postdoc, which helped my career too. She was eager for children immediately; I was petrified, but we managed and had Debbie and Rachel. I realized I had no idea how to parent, so I started reading. Everyone recommended a distinguished child psychiatrist named Haim Ginott, who wrote “Between Parent and Child”, “Between Parent and Teenager”, and so on. They were analytical, but another book—"Liberated Parents, Liberated Children”—was actually even better. It was written by two child psychiatrists who’d struggled with their own kids. They went to Ginott’s seminars, tried out his advice, and wrote about their experiences. The book was delightful and gave me confidence: acknowledge kids’ feelings before trying to guide them. If they come home yelling, “I hate school! I’m never going back!” you don’t reply, “Don’t be silly, you have to go to school.” Instead, you say, “Wow, you really sound upset.” The child vents more, and eventually, when they’re heard, you can gently ask, “Do you think there’s something you could try?” Then, maybe, “Yeah, maybe I shouldn’t yell in French class.” If you immediately rebut their emotions, you just hit a wall. This approach became ingrained for decades. I’d check myself whenever I felt like having a visceral reaction. Even later, as a young faculty member, a student would barge into my office furious about university policies—same principle: listen first, then “don't change a mind, change a mood.” And at home with the kids, changing the mood worked wonders. I’d become a “gorilla,” scoop them up, carry them upstairs, and dump them in their cribs with gentle wrestling. They’d squeal with laughter, then drift straight to sleep. The book’s advice really works beyond children—even adults, as in negotiation, need a mood shift before a mind shift.
Laker: Could you share a few stories about Debbie and Rachel as they grew up?
John: Well, it was interesting—Debbie dated a lot but nothing too serious ever came of it. Rachel barely dated, but then she met Barney, and they clicked almost instantly. We marveled—they didn’t waste time on bad fits. At the first meeting, Barney was sweeping the floor, and Benita was very impressed. Clearly, he knew what it means to keep a home. We just changed the mood, not the mind. With dating, Rachel did her own thing—no peer pressure, no rushing. Sometimes, that works out best.
Laker: Shifting gears for a moment—your cousin Peter Maurer’s memoir’s final chapter is “What is a Jew?” It reflects on lessons from your era.
John: I’m a little embarrassed—I haven’t read Peter’s whole memoir. I’ve read pieces. But it’s a good resource to have. Maybe one day I’ll read it all.
Laker: Well, as we wrap up, is there any last idea you’d like to leave with me?
John: I think it’s important to have some sense of what the future may bring. Early in your career, there’s a lot of uncertainty, but then, if you’re fortunate, you start to have some successes—publishing, meeting people, going to conferences. For a while, the career coasts along—and you don’t realize that, at some point, it will get harder. Health, both physical and mental, becomes more of a challenge. You see the people you’ve trained advancing and sometimes surpassing you; collaboration becomes more important as your own pace slows. For me, the big wake-up call was when my long-term collaborator died. That disconnected me from a big part of my research world.
Laker: Thank you so much for this conversation. I admire a lot how you are keeping on going despite certain parts of your body not collaborating.
John: I would just as soon stop doing my research, but I have this collaboration with a great expert in multiple sclerosis. In a way it would be a capstone on the stuff we've done with estrogen receptors. I have a very loyal Korean collaborator who has been with me for 20 years. He is on top of the day-to-day operations of the lab and some research negotiations. And he says, "As long as you want to work, Dr. K, my job is to help you."
Laker: These kinds of friendships are worth infinity.
