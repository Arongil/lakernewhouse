Joe Newhouse

Interview Transcript - May 2025
Interviewed by Laker Newhouse

Joe: I actually don't know as much about my ancestors as I think the other grandparents do. By the time I was born, two of my grandparents had died. I never met the third. The fourth, my mother's mother, I saw maybe for three or four days each summer because she lived in the southwestern corner of Iowa, and we lived in northeast Iowa.
Laker: This is Maria Logler?
Joe: No, that was Hilda Helgeson. Well, she was really Hilda Johnson (Helgeson was her maiden name). She spoke English with a very heavy Norwegian accent—it was a little hard for me to understand her. And my life had virtually nothing in common with hers; for example, she had little education. I certainly knew her, I have memories of her, and there's a picture of her hanging in our condo. But there isn’t so much to say about her for this purpose. She had emigrated to the U.S. from Norway, settled in a small town in Nebraska—its population today is about 100, and it probably was about that then. She met her husband there, who was a Swede, also a first-generation immigrant. They had five children. My mother was the second of them. Her father died when she was six from a ruptured appendix, and her mother took all five kids back to Norway to live with her mother. But she couldn’t get along with her mother, so she brought them back to the US after a year and settled in Shenandoah, a town in southwestern Iowa. My mother’s older sister died in the 1918 flu, after having had one child and while pregnant with a second.
Laker: Wow.
Joe: Yes. But, shifting gears to my childhood and adolescence, at my high school sports were the big thing. However, I was the youngest student in my class of around 400 students, and as a result I was too light and too young to succeed at football, basketball, or wrestling, which were the major sports at my high school. Mercifully, around age 11, I got seriously interested in tennis. I had good hand-eye coordination and did well at it. When I was in the 15-and-unders, I was second in the state in Iowa. Up until age 15 I played in a tournament in Iowa or Minnesota almost every weekend. I met many people from around the state and some neighboring states who became friends. Starting in high school I taught tennis for the Waterloo Recreation Commission every summer until I went to Germany after college. It was a great job that kept me outside almost the entire day—though my dermatologist would not have approved since I did not wear a hat—but I really enjoyed teaching and could earn some money while staying at home. All in all, summers as an adolescence were fairly idyllic, but I was never part of the in-group in junior high or high school because I was not on the football, basketball, or wrestling team.
Laker: And then you went to Harvard.
Joe: Harvard was a shock in many ways, both academically and culturally. I had never seen a Greek letter, but I was in a math class that was using Richard Courant’s text, which was very rigorous. While my high school was diverse in class backgrounds, it was otherwise reasonably homogeneous, whereas I encountered much greater diversity at Harvard.
Laker: When did you begin to feel secure at Harvard?
Joe: Probably by the start of junior year. I remember as a freshman being assigned 1,500-word papers in my great books class. I would write a sentence and then count the words, because I was so unused writing and I didn’t have enough background to have many thoughts about the books I was reading. At my high school one didn’t have to write much nor do much literary interpretation. And then there were my Harvard classmates, some of whom were clearly smarter than I was, something I hadn’t encountered in high school. Almost everybody at Harvard, I think, had some sense of impostor syndrome, and I certainly was not immune. I did come across the term impostor syndrome until much later in my life, but it definitely applied to me then—as a freshman I wondered if I really belonged at Harvard. But relative to high school I enjoyed being in an environment where one didn’t have to conceal intelligence. By junior year, or maybe the second semester of sophomore year, I had began to feel more secure. I had learned how to study, and I had begun to find an academic home in economics.
Laker: What did you want to study when you first got there?
Joe: I was planning to be a math major. I took the course I mentioned above, which in retrospect was designed to sort out who was going to be a professor of mathematics and who wasn’t—and I was in the latter, much larger group. I thought that if math didn’t work out, which my math course was telling me it wouldn’t, I’d major in science—probably chemistry. So in addition to the math class, I took an honors introductory chemistry class as a first semester freshman. I really liked the material, and I liked the problem sets, but I hated the labs. I simply didn’t have the patience on a nice fall day to be cooped up in a chem lab, so I ruled out chemistry. I had never taken biology in high school because I was too squeamish about the human body, and I never considered taking it at Harvard.
Laker: Me too. Looking back, it seems like missing high school biology isn’t much of a loss.
Joe: I agree, nothing much remains in today’s biology courses from what was taught in high school biology at that time except classification systems, which are boring. Finally I thought that if neither math or science worked out, I’d major in history—I’d liked my high school history courses. So I took an introductory American history course in the second semester of my freshman year.
Laker: What was so bad about history?
Joe: The Harvard history course was quite different from my high school courses, focusing much more on interpretation. It seemed to me that that meant one could simply make up interpretations of facts, and there was nothing testable about one’s conclusions. In retrospect I wanted more certainty than history offered. Much later I realized I was slighting historians a bit with that take, but that was my view then. Near the end of freshman year one had to declare a major. I didn’t know what economics was, but by process of elimination, I wrote down economics as my major. I took introductory economics in my sophomore year. That course, more than any other course I had taken up to that time (besides German language courses), was the first course where I felt really comfortable and understood the concepts that the course was trying to transmit. The course was mostly taught in small sections of 25-30 students, and I had a wonderful assistant professor, W. Eric Gustafson. He didn’t get promoted at Harvard because he didn’t publish much, but he eventually joined the faculty at UC Davis and won a Western Economic Association teaching award there. He has now died, but I am grateful to him for getting me started.
Laker: His father had been a professor too?
Joe: Yes, a professor of biology at Williams or Bowdoin. Anyway, by the time I was junior, I should mention two other people who influenced my life. One was Henry Aaron, who later became a prominent economist at Brookings but at that time was a first year assistant professor who, in a class of six, taught me intermediate economic theory in my junior year. The second was John Dunlop, who taught the undergraduate labor economics course. I took that in my junior year as my first field course in economics because I had grown up in a sea of anti-union Republicans—I don’t think I met a Democrat until I got to Harvard, except a Southern Democrat, who was probably a racist. I wanted to learn about different views with respect to labor unions, which were much more important at that time than they are now. I came to admire John a great deal, because he had one foot in the real world, doing lots of arbitration and mediation of labor-management disputes, and one foot in academia. He was in fact a distinguished academic; he later became dean of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences at Harvard, and then was tapped to be Secretary of Labor by President Ford. At Harvard he ran a weekly labor economics seminar with a visiting CEO or union president who would talk about labor-management relations in his (it was always a male in those days) firm or industry followed by a dinner. In the early 1960s John was farsighted enough to see health insurance would become a big issue in the future not only in labor-management negotiations but also in public policy, so he established a four-school seminar on health care issues, which I audited in my senior year.
Laker: And you wrote your thesis with him?
Joe: Yes. I went to him junior year and asked if he’d be my thesis advisor—I felt like I was asking someone for a date. He agreed, and we discussed possible topics. President Kennedy had just appointed him to chair a commission on dealing with strikes at missile sites—this was 1962, and the Cold War was in full swing. He thought I could write my thesis on that, so he gave me a pile of papers on the topic, but they bored me. When I got back to Harvard in the fall, I told John I didn’t want to write on that topic. So he sent me to see a research associate that he had hired to work for him on health policy. She suggested the nursing labor market might pose some interesting issues. So I wrote my thesis on the nursing labor market, and it turned out quite well. I was taking the graduate econometrics course in my senior year, and I tried to apply it in my thesis. What passed for econometrics in those days would be laughed at today, but what I did at that time in my thesis was advanced for an undergrad. Although graduate students had access to a mainframe computer, undergrads had no access, so to invert a matrix and compute regression coefficients I had to manually punch numbers into a Friden calculator. You probably never heard of them, but they are now in museums (you can see an image on Google)—there were no electronic calculators yet. The potential for a keying error was huge, but that was ignored and just estimating regressions was advanced for an undergrad at the time. I got a summa on my thesis, which of course was gratifying, and the economics department recommended me for a summa, but I had taken a political philosophy course in the first semester of sophomore year that I did not have the background for. I got my only C+ at Harvard in that course, which according to rules that I had been unaware of meant I could not graduate summa cum laude, so I graduated magna cum laude with highest honors in economics. At the time I was very disappointed, since when I found out the department I had recommended me for a summa, I thought for a few weeks that I would graduate summa, but as years passed the disappointment faded away.
Laker: How hard you worked strikes a chord, having just finished my own thesis.
Joe: A second major influence on my life was winning a Fulbright fellowship to Germany after college. Growing up my goal was to become a Rhodes Scholar. Harvard always had the most Rhodes Scholars, which was one reason I chose to go there. I got to the interview stage for the Rhodes in Iowa, but I didn’t get further. I also applied for a Marshall, which could be used at Oxford or Cambridge. In addition, there were a few Harvard scholarships to either Oxford or Cambridge, but I did not get them either. My interview question for the Harvard fellowships was to “discuss the lessons of history,” but I froze and couldn’t think of what to say. Needless to say, I didn’t get one of those Harvard fellowships, but one of my roommate (and later best man) David Oakes did. By chance the last application form I filled out was for a Fulbright, where one could go to universities in many countries. The upper right-hand corner of the application asked which country I wanted to apply for. I’d taken 2.5 years of German at Harvard (which could be another story), but after I wrote “UK” in the upper-right hand corner I thought, “I’m tired of writing why I want to go to Oxford or Cambridge,” so I erased UK and wrote “West Germany.” I knew nothing about German universities, but the Fulbright was the only fellowship I got. So in September I boarded the TS Bremen in NYC bound for Germany. It was the first time I’d been out of the US, and the first time I’d used a foreign language in a non-classroom setting. The year in Germany was a wonderful experience.
Laker: How so?
Joe: Well, although I had not appreciated it at the time, my life up to that time had been quite sheltered. Growing up, I was the only child and doted upon, especially by my mother because my father was in sales work and left home on Monday and returned on Friday. I did some yard work, mostly mowing, but my mother didn’t have me help with cooking nor did I ever have to wash dishes. At Harvard, one lived as a freshman in a freshman dorm and as an upperclassman in a “house,” where one had meals. Freshly laundered sheets were delivered to my room every week. Although I had to make my own bed, beyond that everything was taken care of.
Laker: They did your laundry?
Joe: You put your sheets in a pillowcase outside your door on a given morning and they’d be picked up and returned later that day.
Laker: That sounds amazing.
Joe: Oh, it got even better when I was a tutor as a graduate student in Leverett House—a maid would make my bed with fresh linen every day, and if had had a fire in my functioning fireplace, she would replenish my supply of firewood! I could practice my viola between noon and one, then wander down to the dining hall for lunch with students, many of whom were returning from 12pm classes. It was a very coddled life. When I got to Germany there were no dorms, so I had to find a place to live for the first time in my life. I rented a 17 square meter room from a war widow and took the streetcar to the university every weekday. The good part was that I’d felt pretty burned out after Harvard—I’d worked very hard on my honors thesis, as you just did, and I badly needed a break. The year in Germany was essentially a gap year for me, though I don’t think that term existed then. Germany itself was devastated by World War II, and German universities had been cut off from Western literature during the war. As a result, German economics lagged far behind the US and the UK. At that time German universities rationed their first degrees in Volkswirtschaft (economics) through seminars; one had to take three seminars to get a degree. Each seminar was limited to 25 people, and one had to take an exam to get in. I still remember well the one exam I took (the only one that had not yet been given when my “Grosser Bruder” told me I should apply), which was for a microeconomics seminar. The first question was to solve a linear supply and demand equations for the equilibrium price, quantity, and something called Umsatz. I turned to my German-English dictionary, which the TF’s proctoring the exam let me have with me, and it said Umsatz meant “turnover” in English. I thought turnover was something one ate! So I raised my hand and asked the TFs what Umsatz meant in English, and after checking their German-English dictionary, they came back to my seat beaming, and said “turnover!” So I thought the only thing the question hadn’t asked about that one could calculate from what was given was revenue, and that was right. (I later learned that revenue in British English is “turnover.”) The second question was: “Discuss recent actions of the Bundesbank.” I had been teaching tennis in Iowa all summer—I couldn’t even write about what the Federal Reserve Board was doing at the time, let alone the Bundesbank! So I raised my hand again and when the TF’s came over they told me to “write something about monetary theory,” which I did. The third question was to write a book review of one of three books, all in German, which presumably everyone else had read, but I had never even heard of the books, let alone read them. I again asked what to do, and the TF’s said, “Write a review of any book you want.” So I did that and later found I was admitted. But the seminar assignments were trivial—the entire workload for the semester work would have been a week’s work at Harvard. The exception was the term paper, which of course had to be in German. My German at that point was pretty good, but I made mistakes with articles and prepositions. Fortunately I had a friend who read the paper and corrected the German, but trying to write a paper in German gave me much more sympathy for students in the US who were not native English speakers. More importantly for my later life, the German universities were almost never in session, so I traveled a lot around Europe and matured. As a result, when I got back to graduate school, I was really ready to work on what would become my life’s career.
Laker: It sounds like you were often surrounded by very smart people.
Joe: Both as an undergrad but especially in graduate school. I had a two Nobel Prize winners in my cohort.
Laker: Did you ever consider going into defense economics?
Joe: Not really while in graduate school, but when I got to RAND, I was 26 and married, which meant I was effectively exempt from the draft because the Army wanted soldiers who were between 18 and 25. But I nonetheless got a letter from my draft board reclassifying me 1A, the most likely category to be drafted. I scrambled to see if I could get a deferment. (I’d had a student deferment while in grad school.) The person in the office across the hall was James Schlesinger—later Secretary of Defense—so I asked him if I could help on a defense project so I could tell my draft board I was essential to the war effort. He said, “It’s all or nothing.” So I said, “Okay, nothing.” But it didn’t matter—my age kept me out of the Army. I think being drafted would’ve been a disaster—for both my personal life and my career, not to mention the mortality risk in Vietnam. I still think about that sometimes.
Laker: I think about those questions too, about service. Grammy says everyone should have universal service like Israel.
Joe: From an economist’s point of view, people who want to be in the Army should be in the Army, and people who don’t probably shouldn’t. And the Army also wants people who want to be in the Army. But there’s something to be said for universal alternative service as we now know it. If I’d had a year right after college to do something like Teach for America, that would’ve been fine—though non-military options as an alternative to the draft didn’t exist. But the notion of going to Vietnam—that was scary. Luckily, I didn’t have to go.
Laker: Should we touch a bit on your post-Harvard career? And I’m curious about how you established yourself at RAND, and how things went there.
Joe: Sure. When I got to RAND, I discovered it was intellectually stimulating to be in a different environment. I’d been at Harvard as both undergrad and grad student. I knew everyone relevant in my field, and I knew how they’d react to various questions. RAND had people who thought differently. That was really valuable. At the time, I told myself I shouldn’t spend my whole career in one place.
Laker: You were recruited to Harvard and Stanford as a professor, right?
Joe: Yes. Stanford had always been my ideal location—I had good friends there, including my best man, David Oakes. The professorship Stanford offered me would’ve been based in the medical school with an appointment in the Economics department. I was negotiating the terms of my appointment with the vice-president for medical affairs, since he was also acting as the medical school dean at the time. But before everything was finalized, he had recruited a dean, and when I met him I neither liked nor trusted him. While I was negotiating with Stanford, Harvard had gotten wind that I might be moveable and also made me an offer to be a Professor at the Kennedy School, but the salary offer was about 30% less than both my salary at RAND and what Stanford offered me, so I turned it down. Some weeks later Harvard came back and offered me the chair I now hold and a competitive salary. The chair at that time was based not only in the Kennedy School but also the Medical School and School of Public Health. Later I was also appointed in the Faculty of Arts and Sciences. The person holding the chair, who was the first to hold it, had resigned after two years to be president of a foundation. I thought the Harvard appointment was very prestigious but was having a hard time deciding between staying at RAND versus going to Stanford. Finally I decided to limit the choice to Stanford and RAND and told Harvard no. Later at the deadline Stanford had given me I told them yes, but called back the next morning and told them no. But after 6 weeks or so after that I recalled that I shouldn’t spend my career in one place, so I called Harvard back and said yes.
Laker: Tell me about your research at RAND.
Joe: I got to RAND in September of 1968. A little before then Lyndon Johnson’s Office of Economic Opportunity (OEO)—his agency to implement his Great Society and War on Poverty initiatives—had started real-world experiments in income maintenance or a negative income tax. That meant households with less than a certain income got a lump sum, say $5,000, which was taxed away as income rose. It was supported by both the right and the left, for example Milton Friedman on the right and James Tobin of Yale on the left. The research office of the OEO had become enamored of such experiments for learning about policy effects before a policy was implemented on a large scale. When the Nixon Administration took office in January 1969, a debate broke out within the administration about cost sharing in Medicaid (medical services were free to Medicaid recipients). An economist who had been at OEO was sent over to be on a task force at what is now the Department of Health and Human Services on that topic. He came across a grant application I’d sent in to do research on cost sharing, although I was just going to use survey data. From his experience with the task force he decided nobody really knew anything about the question—it was all ideology. He flew out to see me in Santa Monica and said, “If you’re interested, I’ll give you some money to design an experiment to address this question.” So I did, and my design ended up very similar to what finally happened. The OEO decided to hold a competition to see who would run the experiment. We won, but it wasn’t simple; one had to basically become a small insurance company and run a big survey, which neither I nor anyone else at RAND had any experience with. We contracted with other firms for those tasks. I had no experience in managing a large research team, but I had people around me who did and gave me a lot of help. I was lucky, too, that I got a policy-relevant answer. In brief: cost-sharing made a difference in how many services people used, but the extra services didn’t seem to affect health, unless you were sick and poor. Later, when Nixon won a second term, he abolished the OEO as a Johnson relic, but the research office went over to what’s now HHS, along with my champion, the person who had initially thought an experiment would be useful. There are a lot of stories I could tell that we don’t have time for, but a main lesson was that I was in the right place at the right time. Over the years I’ve written papers that I’m proud of on many topics other than cost sharing in health care, but devoting 20 years of my life to that one project shaped my career, and I could not have done it at a university because it would not have produced results before a tenure decision had to be made. When I was in graduate school, I was sure I wanted to be at a university, and would never have gotten to RAND but for a few random events, but that turned out to be a lucky break for me.
Laker: Thank you. I’d be happy to go for another couple hours sometime. There’s so much to cover, and a lot of stories I’d like to hear. Maybe we can do another installment.
Joe: I’d very much like that. I would’ve told more stories, but then there’d be less substance. But there are many fun stories! And I haven’t said anything about marriage and family! I hope we can do it again.
Laker: Thank you so much. This was really fun.
Joe: Love you, Laker. And I hope you have an even better career than I had—and an even happier family life, though mine has been very happy.
Laker: Well, thank you. I think you’ve had both a happy family life and an excellent career.
Joe: I’ve been very lucky on both counts.
Laker: Luck comes by chance, but hard work prepares you to be ready for it.
Joe: Yes. Luck comes to the prepared mind.
