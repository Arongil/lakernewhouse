Joe Newhouse

Interview Transcript - May 2025
Interviewed by Laker Newhouse

Joe: I actually don't know as much about my ancestors as some of your other grandparents do. By the time I was born, three of my grandparents had died. Actually, two had died and one I never met. The fourth, my mother's mother, I saw maybe for three or four days each summer because she lived in the southwestern corner of Iowa.
Laker: This is Maria Logler?
Joe: No, that was Hilda Helgeson. Well, she was really Hilda Johnson. She spoke English with a very heavy Norwegian accent—it was a little hard to understand. And my life had virtually nothing in common with hers. I certainly knew her, I have memories, and there's a picture of her in our condo. But there isn’t so much to say about her for this purpose. She emigrated to the U.S. from Norway, settled in a small town in Nebraska—population today is about 100, and it probably was about that then. She met her husband there, who was a Swede, also a first-generation immigrant from Sweden. They had five children. My mother was the second of them. Her father died when she was six, and her mother took all five kids back to Norway to live with her mother. But she couldn’t get along with her mother, so she brought them back to the U.S. after a year and settled in this town in Iowa. My mother’s older sister died in the 1918 flu, after having had one child and while pregnant with a second. So that was a big event.
Laker: Wow.
Joe: Yes. But, shifting gears, at my high school, sports were a big thing. But mercifully, around age 11, I got interested in tennis. I had good hand-eye coordination and did well at it. When I was in the 15-and-unders, I was second in the state in Iowa. Then I taught tennis during the summers. It was a great job, kept me outside—though my dermatologist might not have approved—but I really enjoyed teaching and could earn some money while staying at home. All that was fairly idyllic.
Laker: And then you went to Harvard.
Joe: Harvard was a shock in many ways. I had never seen a Greek letter.
Laker: When did you begin to feel secure at Harvard?
Joe: Probably junior year. I remember as a freshman, we were supposed to write 1,500-word papers, and I would write a sentence and then count the words, because I was so unused to writing. You didn’t really have to write much at my high school. And then there were these other students—some were clearly smarter than I was, and I hadn’t encountered that before. Almost everybody, I think, has some sense of impostor syndrome. I hadn’t heard that term until much later in my life, but it definitely applied to me then—I wondered if I really belonged there. But I enjoyed being around people I thought were really my peers. By junior year, or maybe the end of sophomore year, I began to feel more secure. I had learned how to study and deal with the coursework.
Laker: What did you want to study when you first got there?
Joe: Well, I was going to be a math major. I took a course which, in retrospect, was designed to sort out who was going to be a professor of mathematics and who wasn’t—and I was in the latter group. I thought, if math didn’t work out, I’d major in science—probably chemistry or physics. So I took an honors chemistry class first semester, as well as this math class. I really liked the material, and I liked the problem sets, but I hated the labs. I didn’t have the patience on a nice fall day to be cooped up in a chem lab, so I ruled out chemistry and physics. I never took biology in high school because I was too squeamish about the human body.
Laker: Me too. Looking back, it seems like missing high school biology isn’t much of a loss.
Joe: Yes, nothing much from that except classification systems, which are pretty boring. So I got to economics by default, really. I thought, if I didn’t like science, I’d do history—I liked history. But the introductory history course I took at Harvard seemed to be mostly, well, you had to make up interpretations of facts and could almost say anything.
Laker: What was so bad about history?
Joe: There was nothing testable about it. I wanted more certainty than history offered. It seemed people who were really facile verbally did well in history. Later, I realized I was slighting historians a bit with that take, but that was my view then. I didn’t know what economics was, but by process of elimination, I wrote down economics as my major. Economics, above any other course I took at Harvard besides language, was the first where I felt really comfortable and on top of things. I really understood what was going on. I had a wonderful introductory economics teacher—W. Eric Gustafson. He didn’t get promoted because he didn’t do much research—he eventually went to UC Davis and won teaching awards there, but this was later.
Laker: His father had been a professor too?
Joe: Yes, at Williams or Bowdoin, one or the other. Anyway, junior year, two other people influenced my life. After introductory economics, I decided to take labor economics. I came from a sea of Republicans—didn’t think I’d met a Democrat until Harvard, except a Southern Democrat, who was probably a racist. I wanted to learn about different views. The professor was John Dunlop. I admired him—he had one foot in the real world, doing lots of arbitration and mediation, and one foot in academia. He was very distinguished, later became dean of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences at Harvard, then was tapped to be Secretary of Labor by President Ford. He ran a seminar with CEOs or union presidents talking about labor-management relations. In the ‘50s and early ‘60s, he was farsighted enough to see health insurance was becoming a big issue in negotiations. He thought it’d be a national issue. Harvard established a four-school seminar on health economics—I sat in my senior year.
Laker: And you wrote your thesis with him?
Joe: Yes. I went to him junior year and asked if he’d be my thesis advisor—I felt like I was asking someone for a date. He agreed. We discussed topics. President Kennedy had just appointed him to chair a commission on dealing with strikes at missile sites—this was 1962, the Cold War. He thought I could write my thesis on that, so he gave me a pile of books and papers—bored me to tears. I couldn’t find a way into it. When I got back to Harvard, I told him I didn’t want to do it. So he sent me to see a research associate of his, whose mother was the first tenured woman in the economics department at Chicago. She suggested the nursing labor market was an interesting issue. So I wrote my thesis on that. It turned out quite well. What passed for econometrics in those days would be laughed at today, but then it was advanced—undergrads had access to the mainframe computer, but you did things manually, punching numbers into a Frieden calculator—no electronic calculators yet! You’d try to invert your matrix that way. The potential for error was huge, but that was pretty advanced for an undergrad at the time.
Laker: That strikes a chord, having just finished my own thesis.
Joe: The second thing that influenced my life—I’d always been very ambitious intellectually. My ideal was to become a Rhodes Scholar. Harvard always had the most Rhodes Scholars, and that was one reason I wanted to go there. I got to the interview stage for the Rhodes in Iowa, but I didn’t get it. I applied for a Marshall. Those were both to study at Oxford or Cambridge. Dunlop suggested I write his friend at Oxford to see if I could study with him—I never heard back. The application said “country,” and I’d taken German at Harvard, which is another story, but I wrote “UK” and then thought, “I’m tired of writing why I want to go to Oxford or Cambridge,” so I erased UK and wrote “Germany.” I knew almost nothing about German universities back then, but that was the only thing I got. So I went to Germany—the first time I’d been out of the country, and certainly the first time I’d used a foreign language. That was a wonderful experience.
Laker: How so?
Joe: Well, Harvard had been very sheltered and sort of semi-coddled. Growing up, I was the only child, doted upon, especially by my mother. At Harvard, you lived in a freshman dorm or in a house as an upperclassman, had your meals there, your linens—had to make your bed, but beyond that, everything was taken care of.
Laker: They did your laundry?
Joe: You put your sheets in a pillowcase outside your door and they’d be picked up and returned later.
Laker: That sounds amazing.
Joe: Oh, it got even better when I was a tutor as a grad student in Leverett House—a maid would make my bed with fresh linen every day and bring me firewood. I could practice my viola between noon and one, then wander down to the dining hall for lunch with students. It was a very coddled life. Then I got to Germany—no dorms, you had to find your own place to live, take the streetcar to the university. The good part was I’d felt pretty burned out after Harvard—worked very hard on my thesis, as you just did, and needed a break. This was like a gap year, though they didn’t call it that then. German universities had been cut off from Western literature during the war, and Germany itself was devastated. They were far behind. The main way to ration degrees then was through seminars—you had to take three seminars to get a degree. Each seminar was limited to 25 people, and you had to take a test to get in. I remember my test: the first question was to solve a supply and demand equation, and they asked for price, quantity, and something called Umsatz. I looked it up and it said “turnover”—which to me sounded like something you eat! So I asked the TAs what it meant, and after checking, they said, beaming, “Turnover!” So I figured, “The only thing they haven’t asked about is revenue, so that must be it.” And it was right. The second question was: Write a book review of one of three books, all in German, which presumably everyone else had read. I asked what to do, and they said, “Write a review of any book you want.” So I did that. The next: talk about recent actions of the Bundesbank. I had been teaching tennis in Iowa all summer—I couldn’t even write what the Federal Reserve was doing at the time, let alone the Bundesbank! So I raised my hand and said, “I’ll write something about monetary theory.” It all worked out. But the seminar assignments were pretty trivial—a semester’s work would’ve been a week at Harvard. The German universities were almost never in session, so I traveled a lot. I kind of grew up that year. When I got back to graduate school, I was really ready to work in a way I hadn’t been before.
Laker: It sounds like you were often surrounded by very smart people.
Joe: Especially in graduate school. I had a couple of Nobel Prize winners in my class.
Laker: Did you ever consider going into defense economics?
Joe: Not really after grad school, but when I got to RAND, I was 26. By then, you were effectively exempt from the draft. But I got a card from my draft board reclassifying me 1A, highest category. I scrambled to see if I could get a deferment. I’d had a student deferment before. The person across the hall was James Schlesinger—later Secretary of Defense—so I went in and asked if I could help on a defense project so I could tell my draft board I was essential. He said, “It’s all or nothing.” So I said, “Okay, nothing.” But it didn’t matter—they didn’t take me. It would’ve been a disaster—for my career, family, not to mention the mortality risk in Vietnam. I still think about that sometimes.
Laker: I think about those questions too, about service. Grammy says everyone should have universal service like Israel.
Joe: From an economist’s point of view, people who want to be in the Army should be in the Army, and people who don’t probably shouldn’t. And I think the Army also wants people who want to be there. But there’s something to be said for universal service as we know it. If I’d had a year right after college to do something like Teach for America, that would’ve been fine—though I wouldn’t have had my horizons broadened in the same way as Germany. But the notion of going to Vietnam—that was scary. Luckily, I didn’t.
Laker: Should we touch a bit on your post-Harvard career? And I’m curious about how you established yourself at Harvard, and how things went there.
Joe: Sure. When I got to RAND, it was stimulating intellectually to be in a different environment. I’d been at Harvard as both undergrad and grad student. I knew everyone relevant in my field, I knew how they’d react to things. RAND had people who just thought differently. That was really valuable. At the time, I told myself I shouldn’t spend my whole career in one place.
Laker: You were recruited to Harvard and Stanford as a professor, right?
Joe: Yes. Stanford had been my ideal—I had good friends there, including my best man, David Oaks. The professorship would’ve been in the medical school but with an appointment in Economics. I was negotiating with the vice-president for medical affairs, since there was no dean at the time. Before everything was finalized, they appointed a dean and I didn’t really like or trust him. Around then, Johnson’s Office of Economic Opportunity—his agency for the Great Society and War on Poverty—had started real-world experiments, like the health insurance experiment and income maintenance. Income maintenance—or negative income tax—meant everyone got a certain amount of money, which was taxed away as you earned more. It was supported by both right and left: Milton Friedman at one end, James Tobin at the other. The OEO was enamored of real experiments for policy. An economist who had been at OEO, and was now at what’s now Health and Human Services, came across a grant application I’d sent in. He’d been hearing a lot about cost-sharing in health care, but nobody really knew anything—it was all ideology. He flew out to see me in Santa Monica and said, “If you’re interested, I’ll give you some money to design an experiment.” So I did. My design ended up very similar to what finally happened. The OEO decided to hold a competition to see who would run the experiment. We won, but it wasn’t simple: you had to basically become a small insurance company and run a big survey. I learned a lot from that. When Nixon took office, he wanted to abolish the OEO as a Johnson relic, but the research program went over to what’s now HHS, along with my champion. There are a lot of stories I could tell that aren’t relevant here, but the opportunity came from luck. I was in the right place and I’d prepared, and I could lead the project, and I turned out to be pretty good at it—something I didn’t know anything about beforehand. I was lucky, too, that I got a policy-relevant answer. In brief: cost-sharing made a difference in how many services people used, but the extra services didn’t seem to affect health, unless you were sick and poor. Now, I think the findings got over-applied by employers who hiked deductibles too high. But I’m proud of that project. There are other papers I’m proud of in different fields, but devoting 20 years to that was singular—it really shaped my career, and I have lots of stories from it.
Laker: Thank you. I’d be happy to go for another couple hours sometime. There’s so much to cover, and a lot of stories I’d like to hear. Maybe we can do another installment.
Joe: I’d like that. I would’ve told more stories, but then there’d be less substance. But there are many fun stories! We’ll do it again.
Laker: Thank you so much. This was really fun.
Joe: Love you, Laker. And I hope you can have an even better career than I had—and a happier family life.
Laker: Well, thank you. I think you’ve had both a happy family life and an excellent career.
Joe: I’ve been very lucky on both counts.
Laker: Luck comes by chance, but hard work prepares you to be ready for it.
Joe: Yes. Luck comes to the prepared mind.
