Laker: We have records from the Day side that stretch back to the 1300s! Starting with your ancestors, what are some stories you think are funny, worth writing down, or relevant today?

Meg: I only remember two generations, maybe three. It’s so interesting, they were here a long time, and therefore I have a huge amount of academics on both sides of my family. And that was a legacy that, in a way, was difficult for me, because I never felt I could live up to them.

But starting with my paternal ancestors that I knew about—I mostly thought not about my parents’ generation, but more about the older ones. So I’ll start with my paternal grandfather, Walter Locke, who was really important in my life.

He was a perfect example of the rags-to-riches story that was supposed to be America, because he was born in poverty in West Virginia, very poor, hardly any schooling, read by firelight—read the Bible and McGuffey’s Reader, about all there was. It was kind of like an Elizabethan culture then. His father decided, when he was about eight, to move the family to Nebraska where there was more opportunity. He worked in the quartz mines, and my grandfather, as a kid, worked in those too.

But he was able to go to school there and got through high school. He was clearly very smart. At 16, he wanted to go to university, but he couldn’t because he had kind of ruined his eyes by all that reading by firelight. So instead, he became a teacher. Here’s a story I like: He had at least a five-mile—maybe longer—walk to get to the school he taught at, and he loved nature. I don’t know when that started, but he liked being outdoors. Since he couldn’t read for long, what he chose to do was memorize poetry on those long walks. He collected poems and memorized them all his life, and I inherited his poetry book. Later on, it was all hand-typed. That’s one of my treasures.

He later managed to become a writer and editor, first in Nebraska, and then at age 52—he must have been very good at it—the publisher of the Dayton Daily News, James M. Cox, who once ran for president against Harding, invited him to come be editor. So he moved his family to Dayton and purchased—couldn’t have been too expensive then—13 acres of land and built log cabins on them. Three of them: one was the main house, one was for him and my grandmother, whom we called Buddy but her real name was Annette—he called her Nettie. And the third was his study, called Shadow Shack. And their home was called Tranquility. So that’s where he escaped, I guess. 

We, when we moved to Dayton, became very familiar with that property, and that’s when he taught me a lot about nature, and about poetry. So he was a really big influence on me.

And my great aunt—by the time I got there, she was living with us. Her story is amazing. Her name was Ines Celia Philbrick—my grandmother’s maiden name was Philbrick. I’ll tell you more about that line in just a second. Ines was the oldest of five kids. My grandmother Annette was the youngest. There were three boys in between, only one of whom lived—his name was Frank.

So, Ines, born in 1866, was one of the first female physicians in this country. She ended up practicing most of her life in Lincoln, Nebraska. She went around by buggy all over, making house calls, delivering babies. One of my favorite stories is that she delivered all five Sorenson kids—and Ted Sorenson became the chief speechwriter and advisor, except perhaps for Bobby, to President Kennedy. So we had that connection to the Kennedys through my great aunt, and my parents, my dad, and that family. If we have time, there’s a wonderful story about how the Sorensons and the Locke family were fast friends.
