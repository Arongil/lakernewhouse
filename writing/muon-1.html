Neo, The One

"You take the blue pill? The story ends, and you believe that a gradient's entries are unrelated numbers."

"You take the red pill? You stay in Wonderland, and I show you how deep the rabbit hole goes."

Let's start with a thought experiment. If the gradient is 10x as large, should an optimizer step 10x as far?

Almost the opposite:

  • If the loss is $y=(x/100)^2$, the gradients are small, but the model should take big steps.
  • If the loss is $y=(100x)^2$, the gradients are big, but the model should take small steps.

As a preview of what is to come, many optimizers therefore take some form of the following advice:

"No matter how giant or tiny the gradient is, move a fixed distance of one."

But elementwise optimizers have a limited menu for how to take this advice. The simplest way is SignSGD:

  • Instead of SGD: $W_{n+1} = W_n - \eta \cdot G_{n+1}$
  • Do SignSGD: $W_{n+1} = W_n - \eta \cdot \text{sign}(G_{n+1})$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate. SignSGD turns every number in the gradient into either $1$ or $-1$.

The Adam optimizer actually reduces to SignSGD when its momentum is turned off (Anthology, Story 1). With momentum on, Adam's updates are still akin to $\frac{G}{\sqrt{G^2}} \approx \text{sign}(G)$, and each entry of its updates hovers near one.

But Muon... it has entered the Matrix. It does not see entries of a gradient matrix. It sees the matrix itself.

And that will be very useful so that Muon can exploit a more sophisticated way to measure "distance one."

The Fundamental Tension

"When I made the Matrix, I did not force it to choose a measure of size," began the Architect. "I was idealistic then. So for you I chose a norm $\| \cdot \| : \mathbb{R}^{n \times m} \to \mathbb{R}$, and it is how you came to be."

Why is distance one a good compromise? Because optimizers are constantly struggling against the fundamental tension: they want the loss to go down far, but they don't want to disturb the model's output too much; the gradient is only accurate exactly where it is measured. To step a finite distance is always a leap of faith.

But how should we measure how far we are leaping? This is a question Muon innovates on.

Suppose we have single linear layer, $y = Wx$. If we update the weights to be $W + \Delta W$, then how much can the output $y$ change? We'd like a measure of size called $\| \Delta W \|$ where we'd know that $\| y \|$ wouldn't change by more than $\| \Delta W \| \| x \|$. Then even though we actually care about controlling output change, we could get away with controlling the weight update size. So we would arrive at our compromise:

$$\underset{\Delta W \text{ s.t. } \| \Delta W \| \leq 1}{\text{argmin}} \langle G, \Delta W \rangle.$$

The compromise says to maximize the alignment between the gradient $G$ and the weight update $\Delta W$, but not step farther than distance one. Otherwise the argmin would flee forever away like $\Delta W = -c \cdot G$ as $c \rightarrow \infty$.

But what is the size $\| \Delta W \|$?

To see, forget that a matrix is made of numbers. Forget that a matrix is called a matrix.

A linear transformation is all there is, $A : \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{in}} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out}}$. The transformation sends vectors to vectors.

Its inputs are lists of numbers, and we'd like to measure $(\pm 1, \dots, \pm 1)$ as size $1$ no matter how long it is. That's because we'd like activations in our neural network to have entries approximately $1$ or $-1$. It's what $\mu\text{P}$ recommends for consistent dimension scaling, and it's the most expressive range for floating point numbers.

But the regular $\ell_2$ norm $\| v \|_2^2 = v_1^2 + \cdots + v_n^2$ will not do, because it judges all-ones vectors differently depending on dimension. Instead we use the root-mean-square norm $\| v \|_\text{RMS}^2 = \tfrac{1}{d} (v_1^2 + \cdots + v_n^2)$.

How much can $A$ change vectors? The linear transformation replies it will take all vectors $v$ with $\| v \|_\text{RMS} = 1$, and it will bend this sphere into an oblong shape. Whichever vector is sent farthest away, its size will be $A$'s size: $$\| A \|_{\text{RMS} \rightarrow \text{RMS}} := \underset{v : \| v \|_\text{RMS} = 1}{\max} \| A v \|_\text{RMS}.$$

This measure is the $\text{RMS}$ to $\text{RMS}$ norm, the maximum stretch that the $\text{RMS}$ norm can detect. It is closely related to the spectral norm, which measures matrix size in terms of the $\ell_2$ norm: $$\| A \|_{\text{RMS} \rightarrow \text{RMS}} = \sqrt{\frac{d_\text{in}}{d_\text{out}}} \| A \|_{\ell_2 \rightarrow \ell_2}.$$

For an optimizer to scale dimension-independently, $\mu \text{P}$ advocates initializing with $\| W \|_{\text{RMS} \rightarrow \text{RMS}} = 1$. Then $\mu\text{P}$ recommends that the update scale match the weight scale (prior to scaling by the learning rate $\eta > 0$). So we arrive back to our compromise, armed with a specific norm. But what is the solution to

$$\underset{\Delta W \text{ s.t. } \| \Delta W \|_{\text{RMS} \rightarrow \text{RMS}} \leq 1}{\text{argmin}} \langle G, \Delta W \rangle?$$

Bend the Singular Values

The bald boy said, "Do not try to bend the singular values, that's impossible. The direct SVD calculation $$G = U \Sigma V^T \mapsto U f(\Sigma) V^T$$ to send every singular value $\sigma \mapsto f(\sigma)$ doesn't parallelize well on a GPU. Instead, only try to realize the truth... you don't need to see the singular values to bend them. Do you recall what singular values are?"

The boy explains that every matrix $G \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out} \times d_\text{in}}$ has a singular value decomposition $G = U \Sigma V^T$, where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal—meaning they preserve a vector's norm—and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with nonnegative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n \geq 0$, called the singular values. The spectral norm of $G$ is its largest singular value $\sigma_1$.

Another way to see it is we can write $G = \sum_k \sigma_k u_k v_k^T$ where $u_k$ and $v_k$ are orthonormal vectors. Then

$$\underset{\| \Delta W \|_{\ell_2 \rightarrow \ell_2} \leq 1}{\text{argmin}} \langle G, \Delta W \rangle = \underset{\| \Delta W \|_{\ell_2 \rightarrow \ell_2} \leq 1}{\text{argmin}} \sum_k \sigma_k (u_k^T \Delta W v_k).$$

We can argmin each singular vector pair independently because they are orthogonal. Since $\Delta W$'s singular values can't exceed $1$, we use up the budget: set all singular values to $1$. In other words, the solution is $\Delta W = U V^T$ if $G = U \Sigma V^T$. This operation is called orthogonalizing. Finally we multiply by $\sqrt{d_\text{out} / d_\text{in}}$ to recover dimension-independence from the $\text{RMS}$ to $\text{RMS}$ norm. (More detail and full proof in Anthology, Story 2.)

But how can we set singular values to $1$? Materializing the SVD isn't friendly to the GPU.

To bend singular values, there's a wonderful trick that Jeremy Bernstein devised in August 2024.

Cube it! Suddenly, the matrix bends but is still recognizable: $$G^3 := (GG^T)G = (U \Sigma V^T V \Sigma U^T) U \Sigma V^T = U \Sigma^3 V^T.$$

We can bend more odd powers, $G^{2k+1} = U \Sigma^{2k+1} V^T$. The odd powers commute straight into the singular values. We draw on linearity to form up a polynomial, $p(x) = ax + bx^3 + cx^5$, that bends singular values as directly as it bends a single number, without seeing a single one of them. So we do two things:

Divide the gradient by something certainly bigger than its largest singular value, say $\| G \|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} G_{ij}^2}$.

Then again and again and again apply $p(x) = \tfrac32 x - \tfrac12 x^3$ to push every number from $[0, \sqrt{3})$ toward $1$.

Faster iterations abound if we are willing to sacrifice convergence, like Muon's own $$p(x) = 3.4445x - 4.7750x^3 + 2.0315x^5.$$

So we've followed the gradient as far as it will go, but only stepping distance $1$. And the distance is fundamentally matrix-based. Controlling $\Delta W$ this way controls how much each optimizer update can affect the model output.

To recover Muon, we add back the smoothness of momentum, constructing $M_{n+1} = \beta M_n + (1 - \beta) G_{n+1}$ for some parameter $\beta$, typically $0.95$. Muon proposes its core update as orthogonalized momentum:

$$\Delta W_n = \sqrt{\frac{d_\text{out}}{d_\text{in}}} \text{orthogonalize}(M_n).$$

The spoon is bent without having even looked at it. In the next chapter, we'll go through Muon's PyTorch code line-by-line so that you can't get stuck understanding it. And we'll advise on learning rate and when to use Muon.
