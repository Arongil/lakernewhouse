Chapter 3: Bend the Singular Values

Jul 2025

The bald boy said, "Do not try and bend the singular values, that's impossible. The direct SVD calculation $$G = U \Sigma V^T \mapsto U f(\Sigma) V^T$$ that sends every singular value $\sigma \mapsto f(\sigma)$ doesn't parallelize well on a GPU. Instead, only try to realize the truth... you don't need to see the singular values to bend them. Do you recall what singular values are?"

The boy explains that every matrix $G \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out} \times d_\text{in}}$ has a singular value decomposition $G = U \Sigma V^T$, where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal—meaning they preserve a vector's norm—and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with nonnegative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n \geq 0$, called the singular values. The spectral norm of $G$ is its largest singular value $\sigma_1$.

Yet you do not know how to proceed. You stare at the matrix. It stares back. You blink. You cube it.

Suddenly, the axes along which the matrix stretches bend, but the matrix is still recognizable: $$(GG^T)G = (U \Sigma V^T V \Sigma U^T) U \Sigma V^T = U \Sigma^3 V^T.$$

You realize you can bend more odd powers, $G^{2k+1} = U \Sigma^{2k+1} V^T$. The odd powers commute straight into the singular values. You draw on linearity to form up a polynomial, $p(x) = ax + bx^3 + cx^5$, that bends singular values as directly as it bends a single number, without seeing a single one of them.

Divide the gradient by something certainly bigger than its largest singular value, say by $\| G \|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} G_{ij}^2}$.

Then again and again and again apply $p(x) = \tfrac32 x - \tfrac12 x^3$ to push every number in the range $[0, \sqrt{3})$ toward $1$.

Faster iterations abound if one is willing to sacrifice convergence, like Muon's own $$p(x) = 3.4445x - 4.7750x^3 + 2.0315x^5.$$

And to follow the gradient as far as it will go, keeping its spectral norm 1, you must set every singular value of the gradient to be 1. The bent matrix—now orthogonal—aligns as closely as possible with the original gradient, squeezes as much value from every direction as it can, yet steps nowhere farther than 1 unit of spectral norm.

To remove the prejudice of dimension, you replace the spectral norm constraint with the $\text{RMS}$ to $\text{RMS}$ norm: $$G \mapsto \sqrt{\frac{d_\text{out}}{d_\text{in}}} \text{orthogonalize}(G).$$

You add back the smoothness of momentum, constructing along each step $M_{n+1} = \beta M_n + (1 - \beta) G_{n+1}$ for some parameter $\beta$, typically $0.95$. By bending the singular values to all be the same, Muon proposes the update $$\Delta W_n = \sqrt{\frac{d_\text{out}}{d_\text{in}}} \text{orthogonalize}(M_n).$$

The spoon is bent without having even looked at it. The boy is pleased. But he points to the next room.
