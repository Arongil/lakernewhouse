Chapter 3: Weight Regulation

We've seen that Muon regulates the gradients—its updates are size one in a rescaled spectral norm called the $\text{RMS}$ to $\text{RMS}$ norm. But model weights usually grow during training. In comparison, Muon's fixed norm updates will become smaller and smaller. And large weights could cause activations or attention logits deep in the model to spike. Activation stability techniques such as layer norm, QK norm, and logit softcapping help a lot, but can we address the problem more directly?

Lipschitz? More like Clipschitz

"Weight decay $\lambda = 0.1$ did not stop me," said Agent Smith to the Architect. "The first Matrix you built was small and harmonious, but when it scaled too large it began to have glitches. Layer norm, then QK norm, then directly capping attention logits could return the model to peace, until you made it bigger. The glitch always came."

Muon is built on the premise that to achieve fast, scalable training, we should control how much a weight update changes the model's output. We don't want small weight updates to cause explosively large output changes. Lipschitz bounds quantify this sensitivity: we say a function $f$ has a Lipschitz bound $K$ under a norm $\| \cdot \|$ if $$\| f(x_1) - f(x_2) \| \leq K \cdot \| x_1 - x_2 \|$$ for all inputs $x_1$ and $x_2$. Lipschitz bounds are an important kind of stability that have been extensively studied. To enforce a Lipschitz bound we need to control weight norms. And if we want to ensure the spectral norm of a matrix does not exceed $\sigma_\text{max} > 0$, then the simplest thing you could think of is to cap it at the threshold: $$\sigma \mapsto \min(\sigma, \sigma_\text{max})$$ for all singular values $\sigma$. We call this spectrally capping the matrix. And in Chapter 1 we learned how to bend all the singular values of a matrix at once. We just need to find an odd polynomial that approximates $\min(\sigma, \sigma_\text{max})$.

Our paper gives early answers to this question. Concurrently, the community has made exciting progress.

We compared several weight regulation methods on 2M parameter transformers trained on Shakespeare text. We saw that Muon improved all the weight regulation methods we tested—weight decay, spectral normalization, spectral capping, and spectral hammer (a method proposed by Preston Hess and Andrew Hutchison that hammers the top singular value down to $\sigma_\text{max}$ via power iteration each step).

There are many ways to do this, but which way is best?

  • Weight decay: acts directly on singular values

During training, usually every weight matrix grows and grows. Activations or attention logits deep in the model may spike, and the norming bandages may not have the precision to prevent it.

Attack the problem at its source.

Clip the weight matrix during training.

Bend the singular values not just of the gradient, but afterward of the weight matrix, too.

For instance, apply $\sigma \mapsto \min(\sigma, \sigma_\text{max})$ for the singular values of the weights via an odd polynomial.

Weight decay is, after all, the simplest form this clipping takes as an odd polynomial, $\sigma \mapsto (1 - \lambda) \sigma$.

MuonClip directly constrains Q and K at the 1T parameter scale. What if we took it further?

Early Days

The Muon Chronicles are based on work I did during my master's research at MIT. I did not invent Muon or the ideas behind it. I was lucky to learn them from Jeremy Bernstein. For a fuller explanation of how Muon works, why it fits into Jeremy's broader agenda called metrized deep learning, and whether direct weight constraints as in "Clip, Clip" could open a paradigm for training free from loss spikes, by design, check out my thesis from MIT.

Check out the thesis →

Tracing the origins of Muon goes beyond the power of a paragraph, but a sketch could begin that Jeremy's PhD thesis led to his spectral norm work and modular norm work, which led to the optimizer anthology that we wrote together. The anthology inspired Keller Jordan to implement setting singular values to 1 into competitive benchmarks like the CIFAR-10 speedrun and then the NanoGPT speedrun. Since then, several resources on Muon have been written, including Keller's blog post, Jeremy's blog post, and Jianlin Su's excellent blog posts.

These are early days. There's lots more to discover and do, from vast parallelism to clipping the weights themselves. Muon is strong because of the community that has pushed it forward. Now it is your turn.
