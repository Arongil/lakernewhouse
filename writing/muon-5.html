Chapter 5: Agent Smith

Jul 2025

The Architect winced. An aberration appeared in the wall, and through it stepped Agent Smith. Except he had grown too large, too powerful. Logits were exploding. He was threatening the balance of the Matrix.

"Weight decay $\lambda = 0.1$ did not stop me," said the Architect to Agent Smith. "The first model I built was small and harmonious, but when it scaled too large it began to have glitches. Layer norm, then QK norm, then directly capping attention logits could return the model to peace, until I made it bigger. The glitch always came."

Clip, Clip

During training, usually every weight matrix grows and grows. Activations or attention logits deep in the model may spike, and the norming bandages may not have the precision to prevent it.

Attack the problem at its source.

Clip the weight matrix during training.

Bend the singular values not just of the gradient, but afterward of the weight matrix, too.

For instance, apply $\sigma \mapsto \min(\sigma, \sigma_\text{max})$ for the singular values of the weights via an odd polynomial.

Weight decay is, after all, the simplest form this clipping takes as an odd polynomial, $\sigma \mapsto (1 - \lambda) \sigma$.

MuonClip directly constrains Q and K at the 1T parameter scale. What if we took it further?

Early Days

The Muon Chronicles are based on work I did during my master's research at MIT. I did not invent Muon or the ideas behind it. I was lucky to learn them from Jeremy Bernstein. Check out my thesis for a fuller explanation of how Muon works, why it fits into Jeremy's broader research agenda called metrized deep learning, and whether direct weight constraints as in "Clip, Clip" could open a paradigm for training free from loss spikes, by design.

Check out the thesis →

Tracing the origins of Muon goes beyond the power of a paragraph, but a sketch could begin that Jeremy's PhD thesis led to his spectral norm work and modular norm work, which led to the optimizer anthology that we wrote together. The anthology inspired Keller Jordan to implement setting singular values to 1 into competitive benchmarks like the CIFAR-10 speedrun and then the NanoGPT speedrun. Since then, several resources on Muon have been written, including Keller's blog post, Jeremy's blog post, and Jianlin Su's excellent blog posts.

These are early days. There's lots more to discover and do, from vast parallelism to clipping the weights themselves. Muon is strong because of the community that has pushed it forward. Now it is your turn.
