There's a lot of excitement around Muon speeding up deep learning training [1, 2, 3]. But why does Muon work? What is the simple idea behind it? There are already some excellent explainers that introduce Muon's core operation: setting all the gradient singular values to one [4]. The spirit of this choice is that an ideal optimizer wants two things at once:

  • Reduce the loss the most.
  • Disturb the model the least.

But to reduce the loss, the model must change. One way to compromise is to allow a certain budget for how big the weight update can be. Depending how we measure size, we get different optimizers. Two common choices are the max entry (related to Adam) and the max singular value (related to Muon) [5, 6].

But want to see it for yourself? This post is an interactive demo of building your own optimizer. It is based on my thesis from MIT: Duality, Weight Decay, and Metrized Deep Learning. Below are real gradients from NanoGPT, and you see a function that modifies the gradient. Depending on this function, the optimizer is either SGD, Muon, a cousin of Adam, or anything else you can dream up---like the "Reverso" optimizer. Try it out!
