How to MIT

Laker's Hot Takes

The only principle is to do the actually best thing at every time.

1. Don't take prereqs

Take whatever is the actually best class for you. Maybe that is the prereq, or maybe it’s straight to the class itself. Examples: start with 6.121 (without 6.120), 8.05 (without 8.04), 18.701 (without 18.700). Assume that you can thrive in a fascinating class; aim high and drop if you need. You'll learn so much.

2. Soft skills matter more

At MIT you are already technically brilliant; what will matter is getting people excited about your brilliant idea. Learn to speak to an audience. Learn to advocate for yourself. Become inspired and practice inspiring other people. “Take this HASS because it’s easy” is the wrong mindset.

3. Say "no" more often

If you always say “yes,” you won’t have space for the actually best thing when it comes along. So don't join some clubs. Don't take some offers. Like Marie Kondo, ask yourself if a task sparks joy. If not, throw it out.

4. You don't know the best class

The actually best class for you to take is likely not on your radar. Ask your friends for their favorite class. Go to a few lectures. Switch in if you love it. Take advice from older students. Be spontaneous.

5. Listen, but pave your own path

At the same time, upperclassman advice is not gospel. Think from first principles. Aim for personal growth.

6. GPA doesn't matter

When deciding the best class for you to take, ignore grades. 100% of the value of a class is the value you get out of the class—not the grade it gives you. No one will remember your grades in five years. Your first employer won’t care. PhD admissions will care that you exceed a basic threshold. Go for the harder class if it lets you learn more.

7. Sit at random tables

The most valuable part of MIT is not the math, biology, or research skills: it is the people you meet. And everyone here is fantastic, but they won't just blurt it out. Make it your mission to meet people and dig up what makes them amazing. One simple way: at dining halls, sit at tables with people you don't know.

8. Majors don't matter

Take the actually best classes every semester, ones that get you up in the morning and change how you think. Around junior year, look at which major you're closest to completing and pick that one. Decide your major from your classes, not the other way around. It'll make you more flexible and more likely to find where you thrive.

9. Find people who disagree

You learn from people who think differently from you. Listen to them. Figure out how they think. It's okay to get offended. Dare we say it's a good sign. You don't need to seek disagreement all the time, but it will help you grow.
